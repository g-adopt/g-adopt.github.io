1. Basic Examples

1.1. Convection in a Rectangle - isoviscous (Blankenbach 3D)

A simple 2-D square convection problem, from Blankenbach et al (1989), for execution in Firedrake, is displayed in Listing 1. The problem is incompressible (Bousinessq approximation), is heated from below and cooled from above, with closed, free-slip boundaries. Solutions are obtained by solving the Stokes equations for velocity and pressure, coupled to the energy equation for temperature and a suitable equation of state.
We have set up the problem using a bilinear (quadrilateral, Q2-Q1) elements for velocity and pressure, and Q2 elements for temperature, on a unit square mesh. In Listing below, line 3 defines the mesh, with 40 quadrilateral elements in x and y directions. Important constants (Rayleigh Number, \(Ra\); Viscosity, \(\mu\); Thermal diffusivity, \(\kappa\)), in addition to the timestep and unit vector, are defined on lines 5-9. Lines 11-13 define the finite element function spaces and degree, using continuous Lagrange elements, on the given mesh. Test (lines 15-17) and trial (lines 19-26) functions are subsequently defined and initialised, with the UFL code for the Stokes and energy equations defined on lines 28-33. The resemblance to the mathematical formulation is immediately apparent. Dirichlet boundary conditions for velocity (boundaries identified by markers 1-4) and temperature (set to 1 at the base and 0 at the top, identified by markers 3 and 4, respectively) are specified on lines 36-37. We note that pressure is only defined up to a constant and, hence, we define the associated nullspace on line 38. The time-loop is initiated on line 43, with the Stokes system solved for velocity and pressure (z), using default solver options, and the energy equation solved using the same default options. Output, in VTU format is written on line 47, to a file initialised on line 40.
We have computed the RMS velocity and Nusselt number at a range of different mesh resolutions and Rayleigh numbers, with results presented in Fig. 1. Video 1 is the visualisation of this time-dependent problem.

1.2. Convection in a Box - isoviscous (Busse 2D)

Here we highlight the ease at which things can be tried in different geometries. We first examine and validate our setup in a 3-D Cartesian domain, for a steady state case with both constant viscosity -- case 1a from Busse el al. (1994). The domain is a box of dimensions \(1.0079 \times 0.6283 \times 1\). Boundary conditions for temperature are T = 0 at the surface (z = 1) and T = 1 at the base (z = 0), with insulating (homogeneous Neumann) sidewalls. No‐slip velocity boundary conditions are specified at the top surface and base of the domain, with free‐slip and no‐normal flow boundary conditions on all sidewalls. Initial conditions are chosen to produce a single ascending and descending flow, at \(x = y = 0\) and \((x = a)\), \((y = b)\), respectively. The Rayleigh number \(= 3 \times 10^{4}\).
For this benchmark, we compute the Nusselt number and RMS velocity at a range of different mesh resolutions, with results presented in Fig. 2. Video 1 is the visualisation of this time-dependent problem.

2. Geometrical Extensions

2.1. Cylindrical Domain - isoviscous (2D)

The domain is defined by the radii of the inner (\(r_min\)) and outer (\(r_{max}\)) boundaries. These are chosen such that the non–dimensional depth of the mantle, \(z=r_{max}−r_{min}= 1.0\), and the ratio of the inner and outer radii, \(f=r_{min}/r_{max}= 0.55\), thus equalling the ratio between the radii of Earth’s surface and CMB.Specifically, we set \(r_{min} = 1.22\) and \(r_{max}= 2.22\).
Having validated our Stokes solver, we now return to more typical examples and highlight the modifications necessaryfrom our base case. We examine two cases from Davies et al. (2022), at a Rayleigh number of \(Ra= 10^{4}\): the first withzero-slip boundary conditions and the second with free-slip boundary conditions. These represent an extension of of the cases presented in Blankenbach et al. (1989). In both cases, temperature boundary conditions are set to 1 at the base of the domain 1 and 0 at the surface. These test cases allow us to illustrate the simplicity of changing the geometry and boundary conditions in Firedrake. Furthermore, the case with free-slip boundary conditions on both boundaries, which we incorporate weakly through the Nitsche approximation, implies that we may add an arbitrary rotation of the form \((−y,x) =r^\theta\) to the velocity solution (i.e. this model incorporates a velocity nullspace, as well as a pressure nullspace). These lead to zero modes (eigenvectors) for the linear system, rendering the resulting matrix singular. In preconditioned Krylov methods we typically need to subtract the zero modes from the approximate solution at every iteration Kramer et al. (2021).

2.2. Spherical Domain - isoviscous (3D)

We next move into a 3-D spherical shell geometry, which is representative of Earth's mantle. The radii of our top and bottom surfaces are consistent with the 2-D cylindrical case presented above. In line with our 2-D cases, we first confirm the accuracy of our Stokes solver for both zero-slip and free-slip boundary conditions, through comparisons with analytical solutions from Kramer et al. (2021). These provide a suite of solutions based upon a smooth forcing term, at a range of spherical harmonic degrees, \(l\), and orders, \(m\), with radial dependence formed by a polynomial of arbitrary order \(k\). We study the convergence of our trilinear \(Q2-Q1\) discretisation with respect to these solutions. Consistent with our 2-D cases, we observe super-convergence for the \(Q2-Q1\) element pair at fourth- and second-order, for velocity and pressure, respectively, with both zero-slip and free-slip boundary conditions.
Having validated our Stokes solver, we now return to more typical examples and highlight the modifications necessary from our 2-D cylindrical case. We examine a well-known isoviscous community benchmark case (e.g. Bercovici et al. 1989, Ratcliff et al. 1966 , Zhong et al 1989, Davies et al. 2013), at a Rayleigh number of \(Ra = 7 \times 10^{3}\), with free-slip boundaries. Temperature boundary conditions are set to 1 at the base of the domain and 0 at the surface, with a spherical harmonic temperature perturbation for an initial condition.
The only major change required to simulate this case, in comparison to the cylindrical counterpart, as illustrated in Listing 4, is the initial mesh generation. We generate a cubed sphere mesh, at refinement level 5, and extrude it radially to form 16 layers. Note that the quadratic approximation of the domain, for these spherical cases, is incorporated in the generation of the 2D cubed sphere mesh. Additional changes, all relate to extensions into 3-dimensions (e.g. nullspaces and near-nullspaces, gravitational vector) and the initial condition for temperature.

3. Extensions of the Physical Approximations

3.1. Viscoplastic Rheology in a Rectangle (2D)

Our cases are all examined in a 2-D square box, building on those presented in basic examples. Aside from the viscosity, all other aspects of our setup are identical. The viscosity field, \(\mu\), is calculated as the harmonic mean between a linear component, \(\mu_{lin}\) and a non-linear, plastic component, \(\mu_{plast}\), which is dependent on the strain-rate, as follows:
\(\mu(T,z,\dot \epsilon)\) = \(2 \left( \frac{1}{\mu_{lin\left(T,z\right)}} + \frac{1}{\mu_{plast\left(\dot\epsilon\right)}}\right)^{-1} \)
The linear part is given by an Arrhenius law (the so-called Frank-Kamenetskii approximation):
\(\mu_{lin(T,z)} = exp(-\gamma_{T}T + \gamma_{z}z)\),
where \(\gamma_{T}=ln(\Delta\mu_T)\) and \(\gamma_{z}=ln(\Delta\mu_z)\) are parameters controlling the total viscosity contrast due to temperature and pressure, respectively. The non-liner component is given by:
\(\mu_{plast}(\dot\epsilon) = \mu^\star + \frac{\sigma_{y}}{\sqrt{\dot\epsilon:\dot\epsilon}}\)
w where \(\mu^\star\) is a constant representing the effective viscosity at high stresses and \(\sigma_{y}\) is the yield stress. The denominator of the second term represents the second invariant of the strain-rate tensor. The visco-plastic flow law leads to linear viscous deformation at low stresses and to plastic deformation at stresses that exceed \(\sigma_{y}\), with the decrease in viscosity limited by the choice of \(\mu^\star\). Although Tosi et al._(2015) examined a number of cases, we focus on two here. Case 3 allows us to illustrate the inclusion of a temperature- and depth-dependent viscosity, with Case 4 allowing for the inclusion of the non-linear parts. The key changes required to simulate these cases are highlighted in Listing 4 (noting that the full codes are available in Appendix XXX). These are:
  1. The solver dictionary is updated to account for non-linearities, using the Scalable Nonlinear Equations Solvers (SNES) available through PETSc.
  2. Viscosity is determined, as a function of temperature and depth ($\mu_{lin}$) and strain-rate ($\mu_{plast}$), subsequently combined via a harmonic mean.
  3. The solve call includes an additional option: "appctx". This is supplied with the viscosity, which is subequently used in Firedrake's customisable preconditioners to compute the scaled pressure mass matrix.
We also update values for key constants (e.g. \(Ra\))
